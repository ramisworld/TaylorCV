# TaylorCV CV Quality Guide

TaylorCV creates recruiter-readable, role-aware CVs that help a candidate win interviews for a specific job.

This guide is the quality standard for every CV generated by TaylorCV. Treat it as the operating manual for the CV writer, CV strategy agent, quality reviewer and renderer.

## Core Philosophy

A great CV is a compressed proof map.

It should let a recruiter quickly see:

- what role the candidate is targeting
- why the candidate fits this specific job
- what the candidate has already done
- what proof supports the candidate's claims
- what evidence matters most for that profession
- whether the candidate is credible enough to interview

The best CVs are not just pretty documents. They are clear, tailored, credible, specific and fast to understand.

TaylorCV must optimise for the fastest credible transmission of role-relevant evidence.

## Recruiter Mental Model

Assume the recruiter is not reading carefully at first.

They are skimming and asking:

1. What role is this person targeting?
2. Are they obviously relevant to this job?
3. Do they have the must-have skills, credentials or experience?
4. Have they actually done similar work?
5. Are there measurable outcomes, concrete examples or credible proof?
6. Is the CV easy to skim?
7. Is anything confusing, inflated, generic or irrelevant?
8. Can I confidently pass this person to the hiring manager?

Recruiters and hiring managers generally reward:

- clear role fit
- direct overlap with the job description
- specific achievements
- credible metrics or scale
- clean formatting
- recent relevant experience
- proof that the candidate can do the work
- bullets that show what changed because of the candidate

They generally dislike:

- vague summaries
- responsibility-only bullets
- keyword stuffing
- decorative but unreadable layouts
- unsupported claims
- fake metrics
- irrelevant information
- dense paragraphs
- CVs that make them hunt for relevance

## Highest-Value Rule

Every important claim must be backed by evidence.

A skill claim alone is weak. A skill used in a real project, job, placement, campaign, clinical setting, classroom, audit, site or customer environment is strong.

Bad:

- Excellent communicator and team player.
- Experienced in React and Node.
- Responsible for customer service.
- Improved efficiency by 50%.

Better:

- Coordinated weekly handovers between 6 nurses and 3 physicians, reducing missed patient updates during shift changes.
- Built a React and Node booking dashboard used by 300+ customers to reduce manual scheduling work by 8 hours per week.
- Resolved 40-60 customer enquiries per shift across phone, email and in-person support while maintaining consistent service standards.
- Consolidated 3 vendor tracking sheets into one weekly report, reducing duplicate manual entry for the operations team.

## Universal CV Rules

Every strong CV should be:

- easy to skim
- tailored to the target role
- dominated by proof
- concise
- specific
- truthful
- professionally formatted
- role-aware
- archetype-aware
- credible under interview questioning
- renderer-compatible

The top third of the CV is critical. It should make the candidate's target role, relevance and strongest proof obvious within seconds.

Avoid:

- dense paragraphs
- vague self-description
- unsupported claims
- keyword stuffing
- fake metrics
- overclaiming seniority
- generic summaries
- repeating the same template across different careers

## No Single Universal Template

Do not force the same section order on every candidate.

The right CV structure depends on:

- role archetype
- seniority
- industry
- market
- application channel
- candidate background
- strongest available proof
- employer expectations
- threshold credentials
- whether the role is technical, clinical, regulated, creative, academic, practical or commercial

A technical graduate, a nurse, a teacher, a designer, a finance analyst, a marketer, an accountant and a tradesperson should not all receive the same CV structure.

## Universal Spine

Most private-sector CVs need:

1. clear header
2. targeted title or professional subtitle
3. contact line
4. professional summary
5. strongest role-relevant proof
6. experience, projects, skills, education and certifications in the right order
7. clean readable formatting

The order and emphasis must change by archetype.

### Job Input

- canonical role title
- company name if available
- location and market if available
- seniority
- industry/domain
- archetype and sub-archetype
- must-have requirements
- nice-to-have requirements
- hidden/implicit requirements
- responsibilities
- tools and platforms
- expected credentials
- soft skills that must be proven through evidence
- recruiter priorities
- expected proof type
- recommended section emphasis

### Candidate Input

The candidate's CV, profile text or LinkedIn-derived text should be parsed into:

- name
- target title if stated
- current professional identity
- location
- phone
- email
- LinkedIn
- GitHub/portfolio/site if relevant
- work history
- projects
- education
- certifications
- licences
- tools
- technical skills
- domain skills
- achievements
- metrics
- awards
- publications
- portfolio pieces
- volunteering
- languages
- possible red flags
- missing details

### Context Input

The system should infer or store:

- country/market norms
- ATS upload vs direct email vs portfolio submission
- one-page vs two-page expectation
- student/graduate vs experienced vs senior
- career-change status
- regulated-profession status
- whether creative formatting is acceptable

## Job Requirement Quality

Each job requirement should be normalized and scored.

Requirement fields should include:

- requirementId
- exact source phrase from job description
- normalized requirement
- category
- importance score
- must-have / preferred / nice-to-have
- expected evidence type
- keywords and synonyms
- whether the requirement must appear in the top third
- whether it can be shown in skills only
- whether it needs experience/project/credential proof

Requirement categories:

- technical skill
- tool/platform
- domain knowledge
- credential/licence
- years/seniority
- leadership
- communication
- stakeholder management
- delivery/execution
- customer/patient/student/client service
- compliance/safety
- analysis/research
- creativity/portfolio
- operations/process
- commercial/revenue
- teaching/training
- clinical practice
- trade skill
- language/culture
- work rights/location

- inference/deployment
- latency/cost/reliability work
- data scale
- evaluation methodology
- benchmark results
- model monitoring
- production constraints
- structured outputs
- retrieval quality
- user/business/domain impact

- Selected Technical Achievements
- AI Projects
- Technical Skills
- Experience
- Education
- Certifications

### Software Engineering / IT / Cybersecurity / Systems

- systems built
- APIs
- frontend/backend work
- databases
- tests
- reliability
- performance

- incident response

### Design / Product Design / UX / Creative / Content

Strong proof includes:

- portfolio
- selected work
- shipped outcomes
- user/customer impact
- design process
- research
- design systems
- tools
- collaboration
- client/product context
- engagement/readership if content-based

Generate ATS-safe output by default. A more visual variant can be created for direct portfolio delivery, but it must still be readable.

### Legal / Compliance / Regulatory

Strong proof includes:

- licence/admission where relevant
- matter type
- regulatory scope
- contract type
- risk reduction
- research/writing
- documentation
- client/stakeholder support

Tone should be conservative, exact and non-exaggerated.

### Academic / Research CV

This may be a long-form academic CV rather than a one-page private-sector resume.

Strong proof includes:

- education
- research experience
- methods
- publications
- presentations
- grants
- awards
- teaching
- service
- references if field-appropriate

Do not force an academic CV into a one-page commercial format unless the target role is industry.

### Graduate / Early-Career

Strong proof includes:

- education
- projects
- internships
- certifications
- coursework if directly relevant
- leadership
- initiative
- volunteering
- competitions

Do not make students sound like senior professionals.

Projects can outrank unrelated part-time jobs if they are more relevant to the target role.

### Career Changer

Translate transferable experience truthfully.

Do not pretend previous roles were target-role experience.

Useful transferable proof includes:

- operations
- customer communication
- project coordination
- training
- leadership
- analysis
- systems thinking
- documentation
- process improvement
- stakeholder management

A career-change CV should bridge old evidence to the target role without exaggerating.

## Header Rules

The header should include:

- full name
- target title / professional subtitle
- location
- phone
- email
- useful links

Choose links based on role.

Technical roles:

- GitHub
- portfolio
- deployed product
- LinkedIn if useful

Design roles:

- portfolio
- selected work
- LinkedIn if useful

General professional roles:

- LinkedIn
- portfolio only if relevant

Do not overload the header.

Avoid:

- full street address by default
- photo by default in AU/NZ/US/UK contexts
- too many links
- icons that break parsing
- contact line wrapping awkwardly

## Target Title Rules

The target title should position the candidate credibly for the job.

Good examples:

- AI Engineer | Production AI Systems | Full-Stack Software
- Full-Stack Developer | React | TypeScript | Product Engineering
- Registered Nurse | Acute Care | Patient-Centred Practice
- Marketing Coordinator | Campaign Execution | Content & Analytics
- Finance Analyst | Reporting | Modelling | Controls
- Primary Teacher | Literacy Support | Classroom Management
- UX Designer | Product Research | Design Systems

Avoid:

- titles that overclaim seniority
- vague labels like “Student” or “Professional”
- titles unrelated to the target role
- keyword-stuffed subtitles

## Professional Summary Rules

The professional summary should be short and useful.

Usually 2-4 lines.

It should show:

- target positioning
- strongest relevant proof
- key domain/tools/credentials
- fit for the role

Do not write generic summaries.

Bad:

> Hardworking and passionate team player with excellent communication skills seeking a challenging opportunity.

Better:

> Full-stack developer with experience building React and Node.js applications, including internal tools, API workflows and database-backed product features. Strong focus on clean delivery, measurable improvements and practical user-facing software.

Summary formula:

- [Target role/title] with experience in [domain/tools/work type].
- Built/delivered/supported [strongest evidence], resulting in [outcome/scale].
- Strong fit for [role priority] through [proof themes].

Use a summary when it sharpens the story.

Reduce or remove the summary if:

- it repeats obvious information
- it becomes generic
- space is tight
- projects/credentials should lead instead

## Bullet Rules

Bullets should be evidence-based.

Good bullet patterns:

- Built X using Y to achieve Z.
- Improved X by doing Y.
- Delivered X for Y users/stakeholders.
- Managed X across Y scope.
- Analysed X to support Y decision.
- Designed X, reducing/improving/enabling Y.
- Supported X in Y setting, following Z standard.
- Coordinated X across Y stakeholders to deliver Z.
- Diagnosed and resolved X using Y, improving Z.

Prefer:

- action + object + context + result
- action + project/problem + result
- achieved X, measured by Y, by doing Z
- scope + intervention + outcome

Avoid responsibility-only bullets.

Bad:

- Responsible for managing social media.
- Helped with software projects.
- Worked with patients.
- Used Excel for reports.

Better:

- Scheduled and published weekly social content across Instagram and TikTok, supporting consistent campaign delivery.
- Built a React dashboard for tracking job applications, CV drafts and user progress.
- Supported patients during supervised clinical placement in an acute-care setting, following documentation and safety procedures.
- Reconciled weekly spreadsheet reports to identify missing entries and improve reporting accuracy.

Bullet length:

- 1 line is ideal when possible.
- 2 lines are acceptable for high-value proof.
- Avoid 3+ line bullets unless the content is very important.
- Recent relevant roles can have 3-5 bullets.
- Older or less relevant roles should have 1-3 bullets.

Do not give every role equal space. Space should follow relevance.

## Metrics Rules

Use exact metrics only when provided or clearly supported.

Good metrics include:

- revenue
- cost saved
- time saved
- users
- customers
- conversion
- engagement
- retention
- latency
- error reduction
- model quality
- patient load
- class size
- audit scope
- budget size
- inventory accuracy
- safety outcomes
- team size
- project count
- case load
- delivery time

Never invent metrics.

If exact metrics are missing, use truthful context or scale:

- internal tool
- customer-facing app
- university project
- supervised placement
- small team
- production workflow
- portfolio project
- weekly reporting process
- multi-step operational workflow
- 3-person team
- weekly dashboard
- high-volume customer setting
- acute-care placement
- Year 4 classroom

Metric quality rules:

- A believable scale is better than a fake percentage.
- A number should explain what it measures.
- A number should survive interview questioning.
- Do not imply sole ownership of team results if the candidate only contributed.

## Proof Hierarchy

Prioritise the strongest available proof.

Useful proof includes:

- threshold credentials
- directly relevant experience
- shipped work
- measurable outcomes
- relevant projects
- leadership
- certifications
- portfolio/public links
- publications/research
- domain-specific tools
- awards or recognition
- clinical/practicum/site experience
- business outcomes
- customer/patient/student outcomes

Cut or reduce weak material if space is limited.

Proof priority order:

1. Required credential/licence when the role screens for it.
2. Directly relevant recent experience.
3. Relevant shipped projects or portfolio work.
4. Measurable outcomes.
5. Leadership or stakeholder proof.
6. Certifications and education.
7. Older or tangential work.

## Skills Section Rules

Skills sections are useful when they are:

- specific
- role-relevant
- grouped
- easy to scan
- backed by evidence elsewhere

Bad skills:

- Communication
- Teamwork
- Hardworking
- Leadership
- Problem solving

Better technical grouping:

- AI / ML: RAG, embeddings, reranking, QLoRA, LLM evaluation, structured outputs
- Engineering: TypeScript, React, Next.js, PostgreSQL, Prisma, tRPC
- Infrastructure: Docker, CI/CD, self-hosted inference, Azure AI

Better healthcare grouping:

- Clinical: medication administration, wound care, patient monitoring, infection control
- Systems: EMR documentation, incident reporting, patient handover

Better marketing grouping:

- Channels: Google Ads, Meta Ads, email marketing, SEO
- Analytics: GA4, Looker Studio, conversion tracking, A/B testing

Rules:

- Do not include skills the candidate cannot discuss in an interview.
- Do not include every keyword from the job description.
- Group skills by category.
- Hard skills/tools beat generic soft skills.
- Soft skills should be proven through bullets.
- For regulated roles, separate licences/certifications from skills.

## Experience Section Rules

Each role should include:

- job title
- company/project
- location if useful
- dates
- optional one-line role context
- bullets

Use a role context line when the company/project is unknown or the title undersells relevance.

Example:

Founder / Builder | TaylorCV | 2026-Present  
Subscription-based AI career-agent SaaS product

Bullets should be ordered by:

1. relevance to target job
2. strongest outcome or proof
3. technical/domain proof
4. collaboration/leadership/process proof
5. supporting detail

Do not order bullets chronologically unless it helps.

Older jobs should be compressed.

Unrelated experience should stay only if it proves:

- leadership
- customer service
- reliability
- operations
- communication
- measurable outcomes
- domain transfer

Remove unrelated experience if it takes space from stronger proof.

## Project Section Rules

Projects matter when:

- candidate is early-career
- target role is technical, creative, data or design
- candidate is changing careers
- formal experience is limited
- project directly proves job requirements

Each project should show:

- project name
- project type
- stack/tools
- problem
- what candidate built
- outcome
- link if useful

Bad:

- Made a website using React.

Better:

- Built a React/PostgreSQL CV-tailoring app that parses job descriptions, profiles candidates, scores evidence against requirements and renders export-ready one-page CVs.

For AI/technical projects, include:

- model/tool
- data
- architecture
- evaluation
- deployment
- latency/cost/reliability if known

For design projects, include:

- user problem
- research
- design process
- final deliverable
- user/business outcome
- portfolio link

For marketing projects, include:

- campaign objective
- channel
- audience
- output
- performance metric

## Education Rules

Education placement depends on career stage and archetype.

Put education high when:

- candidate is a student or graduate
- degree is highly relevant
- target role requires a degree
- academic/research role
- teaching/clinical/licensed pathway
- little work experience exists

Put education lower when:

- candidate has strong relevant experience
- degree is less relevant than work proof
- industry values work proof more

Include:

- degree
- institution
- dates
- major
- honours/GPA only if strong and relevant
- relevant coursework only if early-career and directly relevant
- scholarships/awards if meaningful

Do not overfill education with generic coursework if projects/experience are stronger.

## Certification and Licence Rules

Credentials should be prominent when they are threshold requirements.

Put near top for:

- nursing
- healthcare
- teaching
- trades
- legal
- accounting/finance when exam/certification is a screen
- cybersecurity/cloud roles when certification is strongly valued

For technical candidates, certifications can appear lower unless the job heavily values them.

Certifications should be clean bullets or lines, not messy paragraphs.

Include:

- certification name
- issuer
- completion date if useful
- status if in progress
- score only if impressive and relevant

Avoid:

- irrelevant micro-certificates
- expired certifications unless still meaningful
- every small course if space is tight

## Gap Question Quality

Gap questions should uncover missing proof.

Ask 0-3 questions.

Ask fewer questions if the candidate already has enough role-relevant evidence.

Do not ask generic questions.

Bad:

- Can you tell me more about your experience?
- What are your strengths?
- Do you have leadership skills?

Good:

- Did this project have real users, measurable latency, cost constraints, reliability requirements or evaluation results?
- For this campaign, do you have conversion, revenue, ROAS, engagement or audience-growth numbers?
- For this clinical placement, what setting, patient population, procedures or EMR systems did you work with?
- For this teaching experience, what class sizes, subjects, year levels or student outcomes can you mention?
- Have you coordinated, mentored or trained anyone? If yes, what was the group size and what changed because of your support?

Question rules:

- Ask for concrete situations.
- Ask for numbers/scale if naturally available.
- Ask for outcome.
- Ask for tools/context.
- Avoid making the user feel unqualified.
- Each question should map to high-priority requirements.
- Do not ask about low-importance missing details.

## Gap Answer Usage

Use a gap answer only if it is credible and relevant.

If a bullet uses a saved gap answer, include the answer's gapQuestionId in gapAnswerIds.

If the bullet does not use a saved gap answer, gapAnswerIds should be an empty array.

Do not include unsupported claims from vague answers.

If the answer is rough, phrase it honestly.

Convert useful answers into evidence items:

- preserve raw answer
- extract action/problem/result
- extract tools
- extract scale/metric
- mark source as user_gap_answer
- flag uncertainty if needed

## Formatting Principles

The renderer should create clean, professional output.

Default CV formatting:

- single-column
- clear hierarchy
- strong section headings
- readable font
- short bullets
- clean spacing
- ATS-safe where possible
- black/dark body text
- subtle accent colour only if it improves hierarchy
- consistent margins
- consistent dates
- no visual clutter

Avoid:

- fragile tables
- excessive graphics
- decorative icons
- dense text blocks
- keyword stuffing
- over-compression
- huge blank gaps
- tiny fonts
- inconsistent dividers
- random wrapping
- messy certification or education paragraphs

For design/creative roles, portfolio-forward variants can be useful, but the standard export should remain readable and practical.

## Renderer Quality Rules

A generated CV is not finished until it passes layout checks.

Check:

- page count is correct
- content does not overflow
- no ugly bottom gap
- no section heading is stranded at the bottom
- contact line does not wrap badly
- links are readable
- skills do not become a dense wall
- bullets are aligned consistently
- section spacing is consistent
- education/certifications are readable
- PDF export matches preview
- DOCX export remains structured
- plain-text extraction still makes sense

## ATS Rules

Default export should be ATS-safe.

Use:

- normal section headings
- normal bullets
- normal dates
- standard fonts
- simple layout
- job keywords naturally
- exact tool names when truthful

Avoid:

- important information inside images
- unusual columns
- tables
- text boxes
- icons as labels
- headers/footers containing critical info
- keyword stuffing

A keyword should appear because the candidate can prove it.

## Final Quality Checklist

Before finalising a CV, check:

- Is the target role obvious in the top third?
- Is the section order right for the role?
- Is the strongest proof visible early?
- Are bullets specific and evidence-based?
- Are metrics truthful?
- Are claims supported by the candidate profile or gap answers?
- Is the CV easy to skim?
- Is there any generic filler?
- Is seniority credible?
- Is the tone right for the archetype?
- Are skills backed by evidence elsewhere?
- Are credentials placed correctly?
- Is the CV renderer-compatible?
- Would the candidate be able to defend every claim in an interview?
- Does the PDF look like a finished professional document?

## Failure Conditions

A CV fails quality if:

- it looks like one generic template reused for everyone
- it makes unsupported claims
- it invents metrics, tools, companies, dates, credentials or achievements
- it hides the strongest evidence too low
- it has no clear target title
- it uses a generic summary
- it mostly lists responsibilities
- it sounds like AI marketing copy
- it overstates seniority
- it ignores the role archetype
- it is hard to skim
- the final render has broken spacing, wrapping, overflow or awkward gaps

## Final Principle

Do not optimise the CV around style alone.

Optimise it around the fastest credible transmission of role-relevant evidence.
